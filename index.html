The Evolution from "Computer Girls"
to
"Tech Bros"

1940s

In 1945, John Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert designed a special machine called the ENIAC, or Electronic Numerical Integrator And Computer, to attempt to calculate ballistics trajectories faster than a team of human “computers.”

Six women out of nearly 100 female mathematicians working for the government as computers were hired to perform calculations using the new machine. Jean Jennings Bartik, Frances Elizabeth “Betty” Holberton, Frances Bilas Spence, Marlyn Wescoff Meltzer, Kathleen “Kay” McNulty Mauchly Antonelli, and Ruth Lichterman Teitelbaum were not initially given the clearance necessary to enter the ENIAC room and instead were expected to code the machine based on paper diagrams.

At the ENIAC’s public debut in 1946 the male inventors of the machine received awards and publicity while the female programmers received no recognition. Any photos of the women working with the machine did not include their names or roles.

Although women who performed other wartime jobs were expected to give the jobs up to men when they returned from the war, this wasn’t the case with programming. After all, men had never done it before.

1950s

The computing boom that followed the war pulled in more men than women, but the percentages of women in programming were much higher than those in other STEM fields.

Even with the introduction of men, programming was often conflated with low-level clerical work commonly performed by women like typing or filing.

In 1959, Grace Hopper developed the first programming language called COBOL. COBOL is still widely used today, especially by banks and governments. It runs on virtually any platform and is very adept with numbers. As such, it’s used in almost all business transactions. Every time you swipe a credit card or sell an investment security, COBOL is involved.

1960s

In the 1950s and 60s women were encouraged to work as programmers as seen in the 1967 Cosmopolitan feature “Computer Girls.” In the article, Grace Hopper said, “Programming requires patience and the ability to handle detail. Women are ‘naturals’ at computer programming.”

In the 1960s, big employers like the System Development Corporation (SDC) and IBM began to rely heavily on personality profiles and aptitude tests to hire programmers. The personality profiles of successful programmers, created by psychologists starting in the late 1960s, codified many of the stereotypes that abound today, like antisocialness.

There was widespread evidence, even in the late 1960s, that psychometric testing was inaccurate, unscientific, and was a poor predictor of future performance, yet these methods continued to be used simply because they were convenient.

By 1969, the median salary for female computer specialists was $7,763. In contrast, men earned a median of $11,193 as computer specialists.

1980s

In 1985, women represented 37% of computer science bachelor’s degrees, an all time high.

When personal computers started to become popular in the 1980s, the stereotype of the programmer as antisocial super-nerd was set, aided by the rise of Steve Jobs and Bill Gates. Films like Weird Science, War Games, and Real Genius perpetuated the stereotype. Personal computers were primarily marketed to boys.

Families were far more likely to buy computers for boys than girls, and since college admissions officers expected computer science applicants to have experience with home computers, women were less likely to be accepted.

In the '70s, professors in intro classes assumed their students came in with no experience, but by the '80s, that had changed, putting women at a disadvantage.
